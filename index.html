What Hasn't Worked

You've tried the standard playbook.
It wasn't built for your brain.

Most productivity frameworks were designed for neurotypical cognition — importance-based, habit-driven, willpower-reliant. Your nervous system runs on different activation architecture. When these frameworks fail, you get the blame. That's the wrong conclusion.

There's a second problem these systems share: they're all built for a knowledge deficit. The assumption is that you need better information — a smarter system, a cleaner framework, a more disciplined approach. But ADHD is a performance deficit, not a knowledge deficit. You already know what good time management looks like. You know you should break projects into steps. The problem isn't the knowing — it's that knowing alone doesn't reliably produce doing in a nervous system wired the way yours is. That's why standard productivity advice consistently fails: it delivers more information about what to do. That's not what's missing. And there's a structural reason these systems collapse even when you start them with good intentions: every standard productivity system requires intact executive function to maintain the executive function it's compensating for. The to-do list requires working memory to remember to check it. The planning system requires planning capacity to sustain it. The reminder requires inhibition to not dismiss it reflexively. It's not failure of effort — it's an architecture problem.

The Second Problem

Something in you
keeps pulling the brake.

Fix the hardware problem — build a system designed for how your brain actually works — and an IFS problem shows up. A Manager part that builds the elaborate plan and then quietly stops running it. A Firefighter that fires when momentum builds and whispers that you can ease up. An underlying Exile whose unresolved burden makes every new system feel unsafe before it starts.

Standard ADHD coaching has no language for this. It assumes the right system will be used. But these parts aren't obstacles — they're protectors doing exactly what protectors do. They learned, at some point, that effort leads to failure or exposure. Until they've been heard by Self, the next system meets the same fate. Not because you're broken. Because IFS protection is intelligent, and it doesn't expire automatically just because circumstances change.

Internal Family Systems doesn't try to override those parts. It approaches them with curiosity — understanding what they're protecting, what they need from Self, and what it would take for them to trust a different way forward. The result isn't a more disciplined you. It's a you whose internal system has actually agreed to what you're building.

The goal isn't to silence your parts. It's to lead them — from Self.

Hardware Layer

ADHD/AuDHD Neurology

Scaffold it. Don't fix it.

Your working memory, time perception, and attention regulation are neurological traits — not personal failures. External scaffolding outperforms internal willpower by a significant margin (Barkley, 2015, d=0.8). Emotion dysregulation shares the same neural real estate as executive function — when one is disrupted, the other is too. It's neurological; not a character flaw.

  • Working memory deficits → external memory systems
  • Time blindness → analog anchors + buffer rules
  • Task initiation blocks → implementation intentions
  • Hyperfocus dysregulation → structured capture rituals
  • Transition difficulty → 15-min buffer protocols
  • Emotion dysregulation → pre-written response protocols + regulation timing
Software Layer

Protective IFS Parts

Befriend them. Don't suppress them.

Perfectionist Managers, Procrastination Firefighters, and Shame Exiles aren't saboteurs — they're doing exactly what they were designed to do. Every part has a positive intent. The protocol uses IFS as an operational framework (not therapy) to befriend these parts, understand their role, and negotiate micro-agreements that let you move. The Striver Part — the one that says "not enough yet, keep going" — was built on a belief that predates your output. No amount of shipping closes the gap it's trying to close.

  • Perfectionist Manager → structured "good enough" criteria
  • Procrastination Firefighter → parts-aware task initiation
  • Inner Critic → unblending check-in (15 min: step back, not suppress)
  • The Overcommitter → energy mapping + commitment audit
  • RSD Reactor → pre-written response protocols
  • The Striver (More-and-Faster Part) → achievement-worth audit + "enough for today" micro-agreement

The Reducer Principle

Borrowed from Rick Rubin's production philosophy: your default move is subtraction, not addition. A Stop/Kill List outranks a To-Do List. The protocol begins here — with every engagement, you leave with fewer commitments than you arrived with. Client adherence at 30 days: 95%.

Reducer Principle = Tier 0 heuristic (not RCT-validated). Stop/Kill adherence = Tier 3 clinical observation.

On Medication + Coaching

If you're medicated, this matters: medication levels the neurological playing field — reducing distractibility, impulsivity, and emotional dysregulation to roughly neurotypical baseline. That's significant. But medication doesn't build the skills. Coaching builds the systems that run on the foundation medication creates. They serve different, non-overlapping functions. Neither replaces the other.

This isn't clinical observation — it's RCT-validated. A randomized controlled trial of 80 adults with ADHD showed that structured skills-based intervention combined with medication produced significantly better outcomes on executive function, organizational performance, and functional impairment than medication alone — moderate to large effects (Safren et al.). Medication creates the neurological conditions for good systems to work. Coaching builds the systems.

If you're not medicated, the scaffolds are designed to work at any neurological baseline — though if you're finding scaffold deployment extremely difficult, that's relevant clinical information worth discussing with a prescriber.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions clients
actually ask.

Is this therapy?

No. This is coaching. The distinction matters: we work with parts at a protector level — building awareness, developing relationship with them, understanding what they're protecting. We do not facilitate deep trauma processing or exile unburdening. If what surfaces requires that depth, I'll say so and refer appropriately.

Do I need an ADHD diagnosis?

No. Most clients come with a diagnosis; some come with strong suspicion and a lifetime of evidence. The program is designed for neurodivergent brains. If that's not you, it's probably not the right fit.

Do I need to know IFS before starting?

No. The language builds as we go. By the time we're working with the model directly, it's already familiar because we've been using it from the start.

How much time does this take?

Sessions are 60–90 minutes. The between-session work varies by module — some weeks require more, some less. Plan for 2–3 hours per week outside of sessions.

What's the investment?

Pricing is discussed on the discovery call. There's no public rate — it depends on scope, timeline, and what the work actually requires.

Is this right for me if I'm currently in therapy?

Often yes. This program operates at a different level than most therapy — it's coaching-scope IFS plus external scaffolding. Many clients run both simultaneously. The more relevant question is whether you're stable enough to be doing this kind of work right now. We'll figure that out on the call.
