Neurodiversity Glossary

A plain-language reference for neurodivergent adults

These terms show up constantly in conversations about ADHD, autism, and neurodivergence — often without explanation, often used in conflicting ways. This glossary isn't a diagnostic manual. It's a set of working definitions: grounded in research, written for adults, and built to make the concepts actually useful rather than just technically accurate.

Identity & Framing

12 terms
Neurodivergence
#ADHD #Autism

Neurodivergence is a term for brains that developed or function differently from what mainstream systems were designed to accommodate. It includes ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyspraxia, and related cognitive profiles — not as disorders to be eliminated, but as real variations in how people process the world. The term matters because it shifts the frame from "what's wrong with this person" to "what kind of environment is this person actually in."

Walker, N. (2021). Neuroqueer heresies: Notes on the neurodiversity paradigm, autistic empowerment, and postnormal possibilities. Autonomous Press.
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Neurotypical
#ADHD #Autism

Neurotypical describes people whose brains develop and function within the range that mainstream institutions were built for. It's not a compliment or an insult — it's a neutral descriptor. It becomes useful the moment you stop treating it as the universal default that everyone else should aspire to.

Walker, N. (2021). Neuroqueer heresies: Notes on the neurodiversity paradigm, autistic empowerment, and postnormal possibilities. Autonomous Press.
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Allistic
#Autism

A term meaning not autistic. Distinct from neurotypical — a person can be allistic and still neurodivergent. Someone with ADHD but not autism is allistic and neurodivergent simultaneously. The distinction matters because "neurotypical" is often used loosely to mean "not me," when what's actually meant is specifically "not autistic." Allistic is the more precise term for that. You'll encounter it in autistic community spaces and writing, where it's used to discuss autism specifically without folding it into broader ND categories. It carries no derogatory connotation in either direction.

Walker, N. (2021). Neuroqueer heresies: Notes on the neurodiversity paradigm, autistic empowerment, and postnormal possibilities. Autonomous Press.
Neurodiversity Lite
#ADHD #Autism

A term for a diluted or co-opted version of the neurodiversity paradigm that emphasizes "superpowers" and marketable ND strengths while minimizing or ignoring real impairment, genuine support needs, and systemic barriers. In neurodiversity lite framing, ADHD becomes "thinking outside the box," autism becomes "attention to detail," and the clinical and social challenges get softened or dropped. The critique is that this version makes neurodiversity palatable to employers and institutions by centering the traits that capitalist productivity already values — leaving behind the people whose challenges can't be rebranded as competitive advantages. Knowing this term helps clients distinguish between genuinely affirming frameworks and repackaged productivity narratives that use the language of acceptance while changing nothing structural. See also: Pathology Model vs. Difference Model; Social Model of Disability.

Walker, N. (2014). Neurodiversity: Some basic terms and definitions [Blog post]. Neurocosmopolitanism. https://neurocosmopolitanism.com/neurodiversity-some-basic-terms-definitions/
The Rumpelstiltskin Effect
#ADHD #Autism

The Rumpelstiltskin Effect is what happens when you get a name for something you've been experiencing your whole life but couldn't identify. It doesn't change anything practically — and yet it changes everything. Having language for your experience can interrupt the shame spiral, open the door to the right support, and let you stop explaining a decades-long pattern as a personal character flaw when it was always a recognizable phenomenon with a known mechanism.

Levinovitz, A., & Aftab, A. (2025). The Rumpelstiltskin effect: Therapeutic repercussions of clinical diagnosis. BJPsych Bulletin. https://doi.org/10.1192/bjb.2025.10137
Twice Exceptional (2e)
#ADHD #Autism

Twice exceptional refers to people who are both gifted and have a learning difference, disability, or ND diagnosis. The two tend to mask each other: the giftedness hides the struggle, the struggle hides the giftedness, and the person ends up without support — or diagnosed only after decades of compensating in ways that left them quietly exhausted. High performance and real impairment can coexist. They often do.

Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Late Diagnosis
#ADHD #Autism

Late diagnosis is what happens when ADHD, autism, or another condition goes unidentified until adulthood — often because the person was intelligent enough to compensate, female-presenting enough to be overlooked, or masked well enough that nothing "looked like" a problem until adult-life demands exceeded the capacity to fake it. Getting a diagnosis as an adult doesn't mean the condition wasn't real before. It means the system missed it.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Spiky Profile
#ADHD #Autism

A spiky profile means your abilities don't cluster around a consistent average — they spike sharply in some areas and drop sharply in others. You might be genuinely exceptional at abstract reasoning and genuinely impaired at something most people do on autopilot. Standard assessments are a poor fit for this shape of a person, and "but you seem so capable" doesn't tell the full story of how you actually function day to day.

Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Masking
#ADHD #Autism

Masking is the ongoing effort of appearing neurotypical in environments that weren't built for you. It includes making eye contact when it's uncomfortable, suppressing stimming, forcing calm when you're not calm, and running social scripts that don't come naturally. It's invisible work — which is exactly why people who do it full-time are exhausted in ways that are hard to explain to people who've never had to do it. Masking is most extensively documented in autistic people, but it appears across ADHD and other ND profiles as well.

Price, D. (2022). Laziness does not exist. Atria Books.
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Camouflaging
#Autism

A construct broader than masking — and distinct from it, though the two overlap. Hull et al. (2019) identified three components, operationalized in the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q): Masking (suppressing autistic traits to appear neurotypical), Compensation (active strategies that work around difficulties — scripting conversations, using external cues, systematically mimicking observed behavior), and Assimilation (trying to blend into social environments by following others' behavioral lead, suppressing autistic communication preferences, performing belonging). The distinction matters because many people who don't show obvious masking behavior are nonetheless running heavy compensation systems — real-time cognitive labor that is invisible from the outside and exhausting to maintain. These are the clients who get told they "seem fine" or "don't seem autistic," often after decades of performing fine at enormous personal cost. Camouflaging is adaptive — it developed for real reasons in real environments. But it carries a cumulative toll: the energy spent camouflaging is not available for anything else, and the sustained demand is one of the primary drivers of autistic burnout. Understanding which components are running most heavily (masking vs. compensation vs. assimilation) can clarify what accommodation and reduction strategies are most relevant. See also: Masking; Autistic Burnout; Double Empathy Problem.

Hull, L., Mandy, W., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., & Petrides, K. V. (2019). Development and validation of the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q). Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49(3), 819–833. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10803-018-3792-6
Pathology Model vs. Difference Model
#ADHD #Autism

The pathology model treats neurodivergence as a disorder — something broken to be fixed, normalized, or eliminated. The difference model holds that ND brains are different, not deficient — that the suffering comes primarily from a mismatch between how those brains work and how most institutions were designed. These aren't just philosophical positions; they lead to different interventions, different goals, and a fundamentally different relationship with your own mind.

Maté, G. (2022). The myth of normal: Trauma, illness, and healing in a toxic culture. Avery.
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
AuDHD — Co-occurring Autism and ADHD
#ADHD #Autism

AuDHD is the informal but increasingly common term for the co-occurrence of autism and ADHD in the same person. It is not simply the addition of two profiles. The interaction produces patterns that neither diagnosis fully predicts on its own, and that can make both harder to identify — including harder to self-identify, even after both diagnoses are in hand.

A few of the interaction effects that matter clinically: ADHD urgency and novelty-seeking can temporarily override autistic inertia, producing a presentation that looks more flexible than the underlying system actually is — until the urgency drops and the inertia reasserts. Autistic routines and preference for sameness can suppress ADHD impulsivity enough that neither looks "classic." The ADHD motivation system needs novelty; the autistic system needs predictability. These two drives run in direct tension, and managing the tension between them — rather than just managing each in isolation — is often where the real work is.

Diagnostically, the two conditions masked each other for most of the DSM's history. Prior to DSM-5 (2013), autism was actually listed as an exclusionary criterion for ADHD, meaning clinicians could not formally diagnose both simultaneously. The research has consistently found that co-occurrence is real and common — estimates of autism in ADHD samples and ADHD in autism samples both run high, with some studies suggesting 50–70% overlap. People who carry both are frequently diagnosed with one and told that explains everything, then spend years wondering why the framework only partially fits. See also: Autism Diagnostic Levels; Interest-Based Nervous System; Autistic Inertia; Masking.

Antshel, K. M., et al. (2016). An update on the comorbidity of ADHD and ASD. Expert Review of Neurotherapeutics, 16(3), 279–293. https://doi.org/10.1586/14737175.2016.1146591
Leitner, Y. (2014). The co-occurrence of autism and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder in children. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 8, Article 268. https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2014.00268

Attention, Activation & Time Horizon

17 terms
ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)
#ADHD

ADHD is a neurological difference in how the brain regulates attention, activation, and impulse control. Despite the name, it isn't a deficit of attention — it's a deficit of consistent attention. You can hyperfocus for six hours on something that grabs you and struggle to read a single paragraph of something you genuinely need to finish. That's not laziness. That's the architecture. Three presentations exist: inattentive (the daydreamer type), hyperactive-impulsive, and combined. Adults are frequently diagnosed late, after years of being told they're bright but unfocused, capable but undisciplined. ADHD isn't a productivity problem. It's a nervous system problem that shows up as a productivity problem in environments designed for neurotypical systems.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.
Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Executive Function (EF)
#ADHD

Executive functions are the self-directed mental actions that allow you to choose goals and organize behavior toward them across time. They include working memory, emotional regulation, task initiation, planning, and cognitive flexibility. In ADHD, executive functions don't fail because you lack knowledge or willpower in the abstract — they fail at the point of performance, in real environments, without the right external supports in place. This is a performance problem, not a knowledge problem.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.
Hyperfocus
#ADHD

Hyperfocus is the experience of locked-in, absorbed concentration that occurs when something activates your interest or urgency system. You don't choose it — it chooses you. It can produce genuinely extraordinary output, and it can also swallow three hours while a deadline sits untouched. Neither version is a character flaw. It's the same engagement system running at full capacity with limited directional control.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Time Blindness
#ADHD

Time blindness is the difficulty perceiving the passage of time that's common in ADHD. The future doesn't feel concrete. You can't sense the hour between now and the deadline the way other people can. This makes forward planning genuinely harder — not as a metaphor, but as a literal perceptual gap. External tools (visible timers, written schedules, point-of-performance reminders) address this where willpower cannot.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.
Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Working Memory
#ADHD

The cognitive system that holds information in mind while you're using it — RAM, not storage. Working memory lets you follow multi-step instructions, remember what you were doing when interrupted, keep track of a conversation thread, and complete tasks that require holding one piece of information while attending to another. ADHD is strongly associated with working memory deficits. The information is heard — it just doesn't stay in the buffer long enough to act on. "You never listen" is both inaccurate and deeply frustrating for exactly this reason. External supports — written notes, checklists, recorded voice memos, structured check-ins — function as external working memory. That's accommodation, not a workaround.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.
Interest-Based Nervous System (IBNS)
#ADHD

The interest-based nervous system is Dr. William Dodson's term for the ADHD motivational architecture. Where neurotypical brains can engage with tasks because they're important or rewarded, ADHD brains require ICNU — interest, challenge, novelty, or urgency — to access full capacity. The ALWAYS/NEVER principle applies: people with ADHD have always been able to do virtually anything when genuinely engaged via ICNU, and have never been able to reliably use importance or consequences as the organizing engine.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
ICNU — Interest, Challenge, Novelty, Urgency
#ADHD

ICNU is Dr. William Dodson's term for the four conditions that reliably activate the ADHD nervous system: something personally interesting, something difficult enough to register as a challenge, something new, or something with real deadline pressure behind it. If none of those four are present, engagement is genuinely hard to access — not because of effort or attitude, but because of how the nervous system is built.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Now / Not-Now
#ADHD

The ADHD relationship with time collapses to two categories: Now and Not-Now. There's no richly felt sense of the future pulling you forward, no concrete perception of how "three weeks away" will arrive. Deadlines that exist in the Not-Now register as essentially theoretical until they become Now — at which point they're urgent. This isn't a habit. It's a perceptual architecture that external scaffolding can compensate for where good intentions cannot.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Barkley, R. A. (2010). Taking charge of adult ADHD. Guilford Press.
Task Initiation
#ADHD

Task initiation is the executive function responsible for starting — the transition from "I need to do this" to actually doing it. In ADHD brains, this switch doesn't fire just because a task is important or because you want to complete it. It needs activation from the ICNU system to come online. The gap between knowing what needs to happen and beginning to do it is real, and it is not stubbornness.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.
Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Performance Deficit
#ADHD

A performance deficit means knowing what to do and not being able to do it at the right time and place. ADHD is a performance problem, not a knowledge problem. The issue isn't that you don't understand how to manage time, initiate tasks, or follow through — it's that information doesn't reliably translate into behavior without the right conditions at the actual moment performance is required. More education about the problem is rarely the missing piece.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.
Autistic Inertia / Behavioral Momentum
#ADHD #Autism

Autistic inertia describes the difficulty many autistic people experience both in initiating actions and in stopping or shifting them. It's not resistance or procrastination in the ordinary sense — it's a feature of a system that, once moving in one direction, has difficulty redirecting, and once at rest, requires significant activation energy to begin. The parallel concept in behavior analysis is behavioral momentum (Mace et al., 1988): the tendency of behavior to maintain its current state in the face of disrupting pressure. In practice this means: starting a task can require far more effort than the task itself warrants; stopping mid-task can feel dysregulating; and abrupt plan changes hit at both ends simultaneously — the current state has to stop, a new one has to start, under the additional pressure of surprise. These compound. Arnold et al. (2023) have described autistic inertia as a distinct, neurologically grounded feature of autistic experience, separate from — though related to — executive function difficulties more broadly. See also: Transition Difficulty; Cognitive Rigidity.

Arnold, S. R. C., Leatherland, P., & Beardon, L. (2023). Autistic inertia: Responses and strategies. Autism, 27(2), 534–546. https://doi.org/10.1177/13623613221092184
Mace, F. C., Hock, M. L., Lalli, J. S., West, B. J., Belfiore, P., Pinter, E., & Brown, D. K. (1988). Behavioral momentum in the treatment of noncompliance. Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis, 21(2), 123–141. https://doi.org/10.1901/jaba.1988.21-123
Monotropism
#Autism

An autism-specific theory describing how autistic attention tends to flow deeply into a small number of channels rather than distributing evenly across many. The autistic nervous system works in a single tunnel of focus — highly engaged in one channel, less available to others simultaneously. This explains: difficulty task-switching, the pull of special interests, why unexpected transitions feel so disruptive, and why autistic people may seem "elsewhere" even when physically present. When the whole attentional system is pointed at one thing, the depth of engagement is real. Monotropism isn't the same as ADHD hyperfocus — ADHD hyperfocus is pulled by novelty and urgency; monotropism is the default attentional architecture, present whether or not a hyperfocus trigger is active.

Murray, D., Lesser, M., & Lawson, W. (2005). Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism. Autism, 9(2), 139–156. https://doi.org/10.1177/1362361305051398
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Divergent Thinking
#ADHD #Autism

Divergent thinking is the capacity to generate multiple possible responses to a question or problem — to resist collapsing too early onto one answer and keep generating instead. It shows up as creative output, non-obvious connections, and the ability to notice what more linear thinkers have filtered out. In ND brains it often runs fast and without deliberate effort.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Sensory Processing Sensitivity (SPS)
#ADHD #Autism

Sensory processing sensitivity describes a nervous system that takes in and processes environmental information more deeply than most. This isn't fragility — it's heightened attunement that makes you notice things others filter out, but also means stimulation lands harder and cumulative overload is real. It's distinct from sensory processing disorder, though there's significant overlap across ND presentations. See also: Highly Sensitive Person, in the Sensory Processing section.

Aron, E. N. (1997). The highly sensitive person. Broadway Books.
Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Transition Difficulty
#ADHD #Autism

Transition difficulty is the struggle to shift from one task, environment, state, or plan to another. From the outside it can look like avoidance or inflexibility. It isn't. For ADHD, it's primarily an activation problem: stopping a current state requires executive resources that aren't always available on demand, and starting a new one requires its own activation cost. For autistic nervous systems, the dimension of distress is more prominent — and distinctly tied to plan changes. The anticipated plan had already organized the nervous system. When it changes abruptly, what's lost isn't just the plan — it's the internal state the plan had built. The disruption is experienced as a genuine threat: the current state has to stop, a new one has to start, and both are happening at once under the pressure of surprise. The emotional response is proportionate to the nervous system load of that reorganization, not the objective importance of the original plan. This is not drama. It's neurology. See also: Autistic Inertia; Insistence on Sameness; Cognitive Rigidity.

Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.

Body Doubling
#ADHD

Body doubling is the practice of working alongside another person — physically present or on a video call — to activate focus that would otherwise stall. You're not necessarily collaborating; the other person's presence creates an ambient signal the nervous system uses to engage. It's one of the most reliable ADHD management tools that no one mentioned in any school you ever attended.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Dopamine & Motivation
#ADHD

In ADHD, dopamine doesn't function the same way it does in neurotypical brains. The issue isn't that you don't care about things — it's that the brain has a harder time releasing dopamine in response to tasks that are merely important. Most of civilized life is organized around importance. The ADHD nervous system needs interest, urgency, challenge, or novelty to move — not because of weak character, but because of how the reward system is wired.

Barkley, R. A. (2012). Executive functions: What they are, how they work, and why they evolved. Guilford Press.

Emotional Experience

8 terms
Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)
#ADHD

RSD is an intense, sudden emotional response to perceived rejection, criticism, teasing, or the sense that you've fallen short — by someone else's standards or your own. It arrives fast, feels almost physical, and doesn't respond to logic in the moment. Dodson's clinical data suggests 98% of ADHD adults identify with it, and 33% report it as the most impairing aspect of their ADHD. It's one of the most significant features of adult ADHD and one of the least named — largely because the shame about having it prevents disclosure.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].
Emotional Dysregulation
#ADHD #Autism

Emotional dysregulation in ADHD means emotions arrive faster and hit harder than most people experience them. The trigger is always real — it's not arbitrary; the response is proportionate to how the situation feels, which can look wildly disproportionate to an outside observer. These states are short-lived compared to mood disorders, but while they're happening they're not subtle, and they tend to leave a significant amount of residual shame in their wake.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].

Injustice Sensitivity
#ADHD #Autism

A heightened emotional and cognitive response to perceived unfairness — one's own, or on behalf of others. As a trait, injustice sensitivity has been studied formally as a stable individual difference (Schmitt et al., 2005), with four dimensions: victim sensitivity (unfairness toward oneself), observer sensitivity (unfairness toward others), beneficiary sensitivity (guilt when one benefits unfairly), and perpetrator sensitivity (distress at causing harm). In ADHD, elevated injustice sensitivity is well-documented: participants with ADHD show significantly higher victim and observer sensitivity than neurotypical controls (Gudjonsson et al., 2014; Boor et al., 2012). For autistic individuals, the mechanism is somewhat different but the effect is real — autistic moral cognition tends to be grounded in explicit, consistent rule-following, meaning violations of fairness register as genuine rule-breaches rather than negotiable social friction. The response can appear disproportionate to observers, but it is proportionate to how the violation actually landed. Injustice sensitivity is not a flaw in calibration. In ND systems where so many things have been unfair for so long, it is often an accurate detector with a very sensitive trigger. See also: Rules-Based Thinking.

Internalized Ableism
#ADHD #Autism

Internalized ableism is what happens when you spend years absorbing the message that the way your brain works is a personal failing. It shows up as self-blame that sounds more reasonable than it is, relentless comparison to neurotypical performance standards, and the deep belief that trying harder would produce a different person. It's not just low self-esteem — it's a distorted lens that gets reinforced every time you're in a system that wasn't designed for how you work.

Price, D. (2022). Laziness does not exist. Atria Books.
Self-Criticism Spiral
#ADHD #Autism

The self-criticism spiral is the loop where a small failure triggers shame, shame triggers harsher self-judgment, harsher self-judgment drains the motivation to try again, and reduced effort produces more failures that confirm the original story. For people carrying internalized ableism, this loop can run fast enough and often enough that it starts to feel like the permanent weather of being yourself. The loop is real. The conclusions it draws are not.

Performance Exhaustion
#ADHD #Autism

Performance exhaustion is what accumulates when you've spent extended time performing normalcy — masking, compensating, managing how you appear in environments that require significant overhead just to function. It looks like burnout, but it has a specific texture: the fatigue of always running a background process that other people don't have to run. The rest you actually need isn't from doing less — it's from not performing.

Alexithymia
#ADHD #Autism

Difficulty identifying and naming your own emotions. You know something is happening internally — there's a physical tension, a fog, a pull — but the word for what you're feeling doesn't come easily. Common in autistic people and in ADHD, and in people who grew up in environments where emotions weren't named or welcomed. This isn't emotional unavailability. It's more like trying to read a label in a language you're partially fluent in. The feeling is real; the word is delayed.

Nerenberg, J. (2020). Divergent mind. HarperOne.
Attachment Patterns and RSD in Relationships
#ADHD

Rejection sensitive dysphoria doesn't stay in the workplace. In close relationships — romantic partnerships, friendships, family — it shapes the entire relational architecture. The anticipation of rejection can become the organizing principle: working preemptively to avoid triggering disapproval, reading neutrality as negative, interpreting a partner's bad mood as evidence of the relationship's fragility, and building elaborate internal frameworks for what behaviors are safe and which ones risk the response that feels unbearable.

This isn't insecurity in the ordinary sense. RSD in relationships produces specific patterns worth naming: people-pleasing as a nervous system strategy rather than a personality trait; difficulty expressing needs directly because the perceived risk of rejection makes the ask feel dangerous; emotional flooding in response to perceived criticism that looks disproportionate from the outside and feels completely proportionate from the inside; and a chronic low-level vigilance for signs of approval or withdrawal that is exhausting to maintain.

For autistic people, the relational picture often includes additional layers: the social communication differences that make cross-neurotype relationships genuinely harder to navigate; the difficulty reading ambiguous social signals that turns ordinary relational uncertainty into a source of ongoing anxiety; and in many cases, a history of rejection, social exclusion, and being misread that isn't imagined — it's documented across the lifespan. The hypervigilance in relationships is often a reasonable adaptation to a real pattern. That doesn't make it functional in adulthood, but it changes what it means and what's needed to address it. See also: Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria; Emotional Dysregulation; Alexithymia; Double Empathy Problem.

Dodson, W. W. (2022). How ADHD shapes your perceptions, emotions & motivation [CME lecture slides].

(Dys)regulation & Overload

9 terms
Autistic Burnout
#Autism

A state of physical and mental exhaustion that builds from the long-term effort of navigating a world designed for a different kind of brain. Not the same as regular burnout or depression — though it can look similar. It's specifically about the cost of sustained masking, sensory overload, and demand accumulation with insufficient recovery. Signs include: sharp loss of skills you normally have (communication, executive function, emotional regulation), complete social withdrawal, extreme fatigue, and a deep inability to tolerate demands that were previously manageable. Recovery requires reducing demands — not just resting. Rest without demand reduction doesn't resolve autistic burnout.

Shutdown vs. Meltdown
#Autism

A shutdown is when the nervous system responds to overload by going inward — going quiet, withdrawing, becoming hard to reach. A meltdown is the external version — the system exceeds its threshold and the response comes out visibly. Both are involuntary responses to a nervous system that's past its capacity. Neither is a choice, a performance, or a manipulation. Both require recovery time, not correction or explanation.

BIMS — Burnout, Inertia, Meltdown, Shutdown
#Autism

A descriptive framework used in autistic community and clinical contexts to map four related states of overwhelm and dysregulation that autistic people commonly experience. Burnout: the cumulative collapse of adaptive capacity after sustained overload — a state of profound exhaustion, skill loss, and demand intolerance that develops over months and resolves over months (see: Autistic Burnout). Inertia: difficulty initiating or stopping actions, related to transition and task-switching difficulty (see: Autistic Inertia). Meltdown: an involuntary response to overwhelm that expresses outwardly — emotional flooding, distress, loss of behavioral regulation. Shutdown: the inverse — the nervous system withdrawing inward, going quiet, becoming temporarily unreachable. These states often cycle and compound: inertia can precede burnout; prolonged burnout increases vulnerability to meltdown and shutdown; a shutdown can extend into burnout if demands don't reduce. Recognizing which state is present matters because the appropriate responses differ. Pushing through a meltdown escalates it. Pushing through a shutdown deepens it. PDA (Pathological Demand Avoidance / Pervasive Drive for Autonomy)
#Autism

A profile — most often found in autistic people — characterized by an extreme, automatic, anxiety-driven need to control one's own environment and resist demands. The resistance isn't strategic or willful. It's an autonomic response: demands, even minor or self-generated ones, trigger the threat system in a way that feels urgent and unignorable. PDA profiles are often misidentified as oppositional or manipulative. Traditional behavioral approaches consistently backfire — more pressure means more avoidance. What works: low-demand environments, genuine autonomy, collaborative framing. Intolerance of Uncertainty (IU)
#ADHD #Autism

Intolerance of uncertainty is a well-researched cognitive and emotional pattern in which ambiguous or unpredictable situations produce a level of distress disproportionate to what the situation objectively warrants. The uncertainty itself — not confirmed bad news, but the open question — is what the nervous system responds to as threat. It appears as a transdiagnostic feature across anxiety disorders and is now recognized as a central mechanism in both autism and ADHD, not merely a symptom of something else.

In autism, intolerance of uncertainty is increasingly understood as a primary driver — not just a correlate — of many features that get categorized under other labels: insistence on sameness, demand avoidance, rigid routines, transition difficulty. The function of predictability-seeking isn't arbitrary preference. It is active management of a system that experiences uncertainty as genuinely aversive in a way that neurotypical systems typically do not. Keeping the environment predictable keeps the threat system quiet.

In ADHD, uncertainty interacts with the time perception system: the not-now future is already abstract and hard to feel, and when it's also uncertain, the inability to mentally simulate it produces avoidance, procrastination, and difficulty planning — not from laziness, but from genuine cognitive-emotional inaccessibility.

This distinction matters for framing: a client who "can't handle not knowing" is not being dramatic or controlling. Their nervous system is doing exactly what it's built to do. The goal isn't to eliminate the intolerance — it's to build enough predictability scaffolding that the uncertainty load stays within manageable range, and to work with the system's need for information rather than against it. Dysregulation and Window of Tolerance
#ADHD #Autism

The window of tolerance — a term developed by Daniel Siegel — is the zone of arousal within which you can think, feel, and respond effectively. Inside it, you can learn, make choices, connect with others, and manage complexity. Outside it in either direction, regulated function breaks down. Dysregulation is the state of having moved outside that window: hyperactivated (flooded, reactive, overwhelmed, emotionally out of proportion) or hypoactivated (collapsed, numb, shut down, hard to reach). For ND nervous systems, the window is often narrower by default — more sensitive to sensory load, social demand, and accumulated fatigue — and returns to baseline more slowly. Dysregulation is not a character flaw. It's information. It tells you that something exceeded your system's current capacity. The goal isn't to never get dysregulated — it's to know what it looks like in your specific system, recognize it earlier in the escalation, and build real pathways back. Nervous System Regulation
#ADHD #Autism

Nervous system regulation is the capacity to move through different states of activation and return to a functional baseline. For ND people, the baseline may sit at higher activation, the swings may be wider, and recovery may take longer than neurotypical systems require. Regulation isn't about being calm all the time — it's about having enough range to function, enough awareness to know where you are, and enough tools to come back from the edges.

#ADHD #Autism

The self-regulation resource pool is the finite reservoir of cognitive and emotional effort you draw on for every executive function task. Sensory Overload
#ADHD #Autism

Sensory overload happens when cumulative input from the environment exceeds what your nervous system can process without shutting down or escalating. It's not sensitivity as weakness — it's a nervous system that processes more, or processes it more deeply, running out of bandwidth. The triggers are real. The response is involuntary. And the recovery takes longer than people who've never experienced it tend to expect or accommodate.

Highly Sensitive Person (HSP)
#ADHD #Autism

Elaine Aron's term for a stable, heritable trait — present in approximately 20% of the population, found across many species — characterized by deeper cognitive processing of sensory and emotional information, heightened sensitivity to subtleties and environmental cues, susceptibility to overwhelm in high-stimulation environments, and strong emotional responsiveness. Many autistic people fit the HSP description closely; the two concepts aren't synonymous — HSP is a dimensional trait, autism is a neurodevelopmental condition — but they share enough of the sensory processing architecture to be clinically relevant to each other. HSP is often the first framework autistic people encounter for their sensory experience, sometimes years before an autism diagnosis arrives. Interoceptive Differences
#ADHD #Autism

Interoception is the sense of what's happening inside your own body — hunger, thirst, fatigue, temperature, pain, and the physical texture of emotional states. ND people don't simply have "less" interoception — they have a different pattern of it. Some experience reduced or delayed access to internal signals: you don't know you're hungry until you're ravenous, or anxious until you're already flooded, or dysregulated until you're past the point where it was easy to do something about it. Others experience amplified or inconsistent interoceptive signals: the body's internal noise is louder and harder to interpret. High / Low Vagal Tone
#ADHD #Autism

Vagal tone refers to the activity and responsiveness of the vagus nerve — the primary pathway of the parasympathetic nervous system, and a central mechanism in Stephen Porges's Polyvagal Theory. High vagal tone means the system can shift efficiently between states of activation and rest — moving from calm to engaged to alert and back — with speed, flexibility, and good recovery. Low vagal tone means the system has difficulty returning to baseline after stress, tends toward chronic sympathetic activation (fight-or-flight) or chronic dorsal vagal shutdown (freeze/collapse), and has less range in the middle. In ND populations, low resting vagal tone appears more frequently than in neurotypical samples and is associated with difficulties in emotional regulation, social engagement, and physiological recovery from stress. Vagal tone is not fixed: it responds to predictable, safe relationships; slow diaphragmatic breathing; rhythmic movement; and trauma-informed care over time. It is a measure of nervous system flexibility, not character.

Sleep Differences
#ADHD #Autism

Sleep disruption is among the most common and most consistently underaddressed features of neurodivergent experience. Research across ADHD and autism samples consistently finds elevated rates of sleep difficulty — with some estimates suggesting 50–80% of autistic people and 25–50% of people with ADHD experience clinically significant sleep problems, compared to roughly 10–30% of the general population.

The mechanisms differ by profile. In ADHD, delayed sleep phase is particularly common: the circadian rhythm runs late, making it genuinely difficult to fall asleep at conventional times and to wake early without significant impairment. This isn't a failure of sleep hygiene — it's a biological timing difference. The ADHD brain also struggles to disengage from stimulation, making the transition to sleep an executive function problem as much as a physiological one.

In autism, sleep architecture itself is often different: lighter sleep, reduced REM, more frequent night waking, and difficulty returning to sleep after waking. Sensory sensitivity affects the sleep environment — small sounds, light, temperature variation, and the physical sensation of bedding all carry more signal than they do for neurotypical sleepers.

The downstream effects are direct and severe. Sleep deprivation in ND systems doesn't just produce tiredness — it narrows the window of tolerance, increases emotional reactivity, depletes the self-regulation resource pool, and exacerbates every executive function difficulty. Poor sleep is frequently misread as worsening ADHD or mood instability when it is the primary driver of both. Addressing it isn't optional self-care. It's foundational. Sensory-Seeking
#ADHD #Autism

Sensory processing differences don't only show up as avoidance. Many ND people actively seek out intense sensory input — not in spite of a sensitive nervous system, but because of it. Craving loud music, strong flavors, physical pressure, rough textures, intense movement, or high-stimulation environments isn't the opposite of sensory sensitivity. Dunn's (1997) Sensory Processing Framework identifies seeking as one of four sensory response patterns — alongside avoiding, sensitivity, and registration — each reflecting a different combination of neurological threshold and behavioral response. Seeking behavior reflects a high neurological threshold: the system needs more input than average to register sensation as salient.

Seeking and avoiding can also coexist in the same person across different sensory channels: overwhelming in one modality, under-responsive in another. Both are regulatory strategies. Sensory Gating
#Autism

The neurological process by which the brain filters sensory information before it reaches conscious awareness — screening out input deemed redundant so that attention can focus on what's novel or relevant. In neurotypical processing, sensory gating operates automatically and efficiently: background noise, repeated sounds, and peripheral movement get pre-filtered, never entering conscious experience. In many autistic brains, sensory gating is less effective — more information gets through the filter. The result is that autistic perception is genuinely richer in information: more arrives, less is screened, and what would be background for others is foreground. This is the neurological mechanism underlying much of what gets labeled autistic sensory sensitivity. The richness of the experience and the overwhelm come from the same source. Autistic perception is not broken. It is a different configuration of the filter — one that admits more of the world, at a corresponding processing cost. Research using prepulse inhibition paradigms and auditory evoked potential studies has consistently documented reduced sensory gating in autistic populations. #Autism

The Double Empathy Problem
#Autism

A theory proposed by autistic researcher Damian Milton (2012) that fundamentally reframes how we understand autistic social difficulty. The standard model of autism locates the problem inside the autistic brain: insufficient theory of mind, reduced empathy, impaired social cognition. The Double Empathy Problem argues this framing is structurally wrong. When autistic and non-autistic people interact, both parties experience difficulty understanding and predicting each other. The non-autistic person struggles to read the autistic person just as genuinely as the reverse — but because the majority group has been designated as the standard, only one party's difficulty gets named as a deficit. Research throughout the 2010s and 2020s has increasingly supported this: autistic people communicate effectively with each other; the breakdown happens specifically across neurotypes. This reframes autism-specific social differences as a mismatch phenomenon rather than a one-sided autistic deficit. The clinical and coaching implication is significant — it shifts the question from "what's wrong with this person's social brain" to "what kind of environment and what kind of interlocutors is this person in." Many clients have spent years believing they are fundamentally broken at human connection. The Double Empathy Problem doesn't resolve the difficulty, but it relocates its source. See also: Brain Differences in Social Processing (Autism); Cognitive Empathy vs. Affective Empathy.

Theory of Mind
#Autism

The cognitive capacity to attribute mental states — beliefs, intentions, desires, knowledge — to other people, and to recognize that others have inner experiences that differ from your own. Baron-Cohen, Leslie, and Frith's (1985) "Sally-Anne" false belief task found that autistic children frequently failed to attribute false beliefs to characters in a story, responding based on what is true rather than what the character believes. This was interpreted as evidence of impaired Theory of Mind — "mind-blindness" — and became one of the most influential framings in autism research and clinical culture. The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) complicates this significantly: non-autistic people also fail to accurately attribute mental states to autistic people. The comprehension gap runs in both directions. Framing it as solely an autistic deficit is accurate only if the neurotypical standard is the universal norm. What the research actually shows is two different processing styles that don't read each other accurately — not one broken style and one functioning one. For clients who carry the "I can't understand people" self-narrative that Theory of Mind framing produces: that story is incomplete. Most autistic people understand autistic people quite well. The difficulty is specific to cross-neurotype interaction, and it is not theirs alone to carry. See also: The Double Empathy Problem; Cognitive Empathy vs. Affective Empathy.

Social Communication Differences (including Literalism)
#Autism

Autistic communication is not a broken version of neurotypical communication. It is a different system — coherent, consistent, and well-suited to direct information exchange — that operates by different conventions. The friction that arises in cross-neurotype interaction comes from the mismatch between two systems, not from a deficit in one of them.

Literalism. Autistic communication tends toward taking language at face value — processing what words mean rather than inferring what the speaker might have meant instead. "Can you pass the salt?" registers as a question about capacity, not a request. "We should get coffee sometime" registers as a genuine proposal requiring follow-through, not a social nicety. This isn't a failure to understand nuance in the abstract — it's a communication system built around the reasonable assumption that people say what they mean. In environments where they often don't, that assumption creates friction. The friction belongs to the mismatch, not to the person who assumed honesty.

Directness. Autistic communication tends to skip the social scaffolding that neurotypical conventions use to soften, hedge, or indirect a message. This is frequently read as rudeness or social obliviousness. It is usually none of those things. It is the absence of a layer of performance that the speaker doesn't experience as necessary — because in a communication system built around clarity, it isn't.

Scripting. Many autistic people develop internal scripts for navigating social situations — prepared language for greetings, transitions, small talk, and interactions that would otherwise require real-time social processing under load. Scripts reduce cognitive overhead. When the script runs out or the situation diverges from what was anticipated, the load increases sharply.

Prosody and non-verbal differences. Autistic speech often differs in rhythm, cadence, volume modulation, or intonation in ways that read as flat, overly formal, or emotionally mismatched to neurotypical listeners. These differences are in the delivery, not in the underlying emotional content.

Recognizing these as a coherent system — rather than a collection of social errors — changes the clinical frame considerably. The goal is not to coach autistic people into neurotypical communication. It is to help them understand their own system, communicate across the neurotype gap when necessary, and stop carrying shame for a communication style that works fine in the right environment. Social Vulnerability and Autistic Trust
#Autism

Autistic people are statistically more likely to be taken advantage of, deceived, manipulated, or exploited in social and professional contexts — and the mechanism isn't gullibility in the pejorative sense. It's the predictable consequence of operating from a high-integrity communication model in a world where a significant portion of social interaction runs on strategic impression management, indirect signaling, and unstated agendas.

The autistic communication system is built around a foundational assumption: that people mean what they say. This is a coherent, internally consistent position. It produces directness, honesty, and a degree of transparency that many people find disarming — because most social environments don't default to it. The problem isn't the assumption. The problem is the asymmetry: the autistic person is playing with their cards face-up in a game where many other players are holding theirs close and reading the table.

Several overlapping features contribute to this vulnerability. Literalism means that reassurances, promises, and stated intentions are taken at face value rather than filtered through skepticism about motive. Reduced cognitive empathy — specifically, the difficulty modeling what another person might be concealing or strategically presenting — means hidden agendas are harder to detect. The strong internal pull toward fairness and consistency makes it harder to anticipate that others may not be operating from the same framework. And a lifetime of social confusion can produce a learned deference to others' social interpretations that creates its own vulnerability.

What gets misread as naivety is often something more precise: a values system that prioritizes honesty and consistency, combined with a processing style that isn't optimized for detecting deception. The goal isn't to make autistic people more suspicious in ways that compromise their integrity. It's to build enough pattern recognition that they can identify when the environment is operating by rules that differ from their own — and adjust accordingly, without having to abandon who they are. Selective Mutism
#Autism

Selective mutism is a condition in which a person is capable of speech in some contexts and functionally unable to produce it in others — typically high-anxiety or unfamiliar social situations. It is not a choice, a refusal, or a performance. The speech mechanism is intact; what fails is the access to it under specific conditions of threat or overwhelm. The person knows what they want to say. The words don't come.

In children, selective mutism is most commonly identified in school settings and classified as an anxiety disorder. In adults — particularly autistic adults — it is frequently unrecognized, because it either went undiagnosed in childhood or presents differently: as sudden loss of speech during conflict, sensory overload, or emotional flooding; as the inability to speak on the phone even when speaking in person is manageable; as going quiet in group settings while being fully articulate one-on-one. It often intersects with autistic shutdown states — the two can be difficult to distinguish because they produce the same outward presentation and often co-occur.

The social cost of adult selective mutism is significant and largely invisible. People who go nonverbal in meetings, during arguments, or on calls are frequently read as withholding, passive-aggressive, or disengaged. They are none of those things. They are in a state where speech is genuinely inaccessible. Alternative communication — text, writing, typing — often works when speech doesn't, because it bypasses the specific access point that's blocked. Knowing that in advance, and having a plan for it, changes the experience considerably. See also: Shutdown vs. Meltdown; BIMS; Autistic Burnout.

Echolalia
#Autism

Echolalia is the repetition of words, phrases, or longer passages of speech — either immediately after hearing them or retrieved from memory and used later. In children it is often identified as a communication difference; in adults it is rarely named at all, which means many autistic adults have been doing it their whole lives without knowing it had a term or a function.

Two forms: immediate echolalia repeats something just heard, often as a processing response, a confirmation, or a way of holding language in the system while meaning catches up. Delayed echolalia draws on stored phrases — from television, books, conversations, scripts — and deploys them in contexts where they fit, sometimes precisely and sometimes approximately. The phrase isn't random. It carries meaning that was attached to it when it was first encoded, and the autistic speaker is using it to communicate something real. The communication is happening. The vehicle is just different from what the listener expected.

Echolalia in adults is frequently misread. Repeating part of what someone just said sounds like mockery or inattention. Using a memorized phrase in an emotionally loaded moment sounds like deflection or affect avoidance. Neither interpretation is accurate. For many autistic people, echolalic speech is functional, expressive, and sometimes the most precise communication available — particularly under stress, when generative language is harder to access. Cognitive Empathy vs. Affective Empathy
#Autism

A clinically important distinction that the phrase "autistic people lack empathy" consistently fails to make. Cognitive empathy is the capacity to model what another person is thinking or feeling — to infer their inner state from social cues, facial expression, tone, context. It is closely tied to Theory of Mind. Affective empathy is actually feeling with another person: caring about their pain, experiencing resonance with their emotional state, being moved by what moves them. These are not the same thing, and they do not move together in autism. Research consistently finds reduced cognitive empathy in many autistic people — the machinery for reading and modeling neurotypical social signals is genuinely different. Affective empathy is a different story: autistic people often report feeling others' emotional states intensely, sometimes overwhelmingly so. Some researchers describe a pattern of hyper-empathy in autism — not too little feeling, but too much, without the social modeling system to make sense of it. Baron-Cohen's work distinguishes these explicitly: his Empathy Quotient (EQ) measures cognitive empathy specifically, and he is precise that low EQ in autism does not indicate reduced caring or emotional responsiveness. The stereotype of the cold, unfeeling autistic person is built on conflating the two. Clinically: when a client says they don't understand why people feel what they feel, that's cognitive empathy. When they say they feel everything too hard and don't know how to manage it, that's affective empathy — and it's a different problem entirely. See also: Double Empathy Problem; Theory of Mind; Systemizing.

Brain Differences in Social Processing (Autism)
#Autism

Neuroimaging research has identified consistent differences in how autistic brains process social information. Reduced activation in the fusiform face area — typically engaged during face recognition — has been replicated across multiple studies (Schultz et al., 2000). The amygdala shows both hyperactivation in response to social stimuli and structural differences in many autistic samples (Baron-Cohen et al., 2000). Connectivity within the "social brain network" — including the temporal-parietal junction, medial prefrontal cortex, and superior temporal sulcus, regions involved in reading intentions and mental states — is characteristically different. These findings have been widely interpreted as evidence of social processing deficits. Damian Milton's Double Empathy Problem (2012) complicates that framing significantly: autistic people process social information differently, but communicate effectively with each other. The difficulties are most pronounced in cross-neurotype interactions — which means the problem is bidirectional, not localized to the autistic brain. This reframes autism-specific social differences as a mismatch phenomenon rather than a one-sided autistic deficit. The brain is not broken. It is different. The environment — built for a different kind of social processor — is where the friction lives.

Systemizing
#Autism

Systemizing is the drive to analyze, build, and understand rule-based systems — mechanisms, patterns, categories, and how things actually work underneath. It shows up prominently in autistic cognition. Baron-Cohen's empathizing-systemizing theory positions high systemizing as a defining feature of autistic cognitive style — and as a genuine strength when the domain rewards it. At its most challenging, it can collide with the parts of social life that don't follow rules and actively resist being systematized.

Weak Central Coherence
#Autism

A cognitive theory of autism proposed by Uta Frith (1989) describing a processing style that tends toward detail over gestalt — parts before wholes. Where most brains automatically integrate sensory and cognitive information into contextualized wholes (strong central coherence), this processing style maintains a more local, detail-focused approach: noticing exactly which element is out of place in a complex scene, remembering specifics that others filtered, building knowledge in granular and precise form. The "weak" framing is misleading — what's described is better understood as a different processing priority than a deficit. Frith and Happé later updated the theory toward a "central coherence continuum" rather than a binary. The Enhanced Perceptual Functioning model (Mottron & Dawson, 2006) offers a more affirming reframe of the same underlying observations: the detail-focused processing isn't a failure to integrate — it's a different configuration of perceptual strength. Insistence on Sameness
#Autism

A term from the DSM-5 (APA, 2013) describing one of the central manifestations of autism's Restricted and Repetitive Behaviors domain: a strong, often anxiety-driven need for predictability — in routines, environments, sequences, and plans. Cognitive Rigidity
#Autism

Difficulty shifting from one cognitive frame, expectation, or plan to another — especially when the shift is sudden or externally imposed. Cognitive rigidity reflects a real pattern in autistic neural processing: the executive function of cognitive flexibility (the ability to release one organizing framework and adopt a new one) is genuinely compromised in many autistic presentations. Special Interest
#Autism

The deep, sustained, and often encyclopedic engagement with a specific topic, domain, or activity that characterizes many autistic people's experience. Savant Syndrome
#Autism

A phenomenon in which exceptional skill — sometimes far beyond what any non-savant achieves — emerges in a specific, often narrow domain alongside significant developmental or cognitive difference. Rules-Based Thinking
#ADHD #Autism

A cognitive style in which moral, social, and practical reasoning is organized around explicit, consistent rules rather than implicit, context-dependent social norms. Prominent in autistic cognition and common in ADHD. Rules-based thinkers often find arbitrary or inconsistently applied social conventions genuinely confusing or distressing — not because they lack empathy or social awareness, but because the rules don't hold consistently and the inconsistency is itself the problem. They tend to apply standards with greater evenness than neurotypical peers, holding themselves and others to the same criteria regardless of social hierarchy or relational proximity. Baron-Cohen's systemizing theory (2002) is partly about this: the drive to understand the underlying rule-based logic of things, including social systems. This is a genuine strength in domains where consistent principle application matters — law, ethics, science, design. It creates friction in social environments where the unspoken rules are unstated, flexible, and understood to be unevenly applied. Many rules-based thinkers have spent years being told they're "too literal," "too rigid," or "taking things too seriously" — when in fact they were applying the actual stated rules, and confused that others weren't. Diagnostic Context

4 terms
Autism Diagnostic Levels (DSM-5)
#Autism

The DSM-5 (APA, 2013) replaced the previous system of separate autism subtypes — Autistic Disorder, Asperger's Syndrome, PDD-NOS — with a single diagnosis: Autism Spectrum Disorder, organized into three support levels based on the degree of support a person requires in social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviors.

Level 1 ("requiring support"): Noticeable differences in social communication that cause real difficulty without support, but the person can function in many environments with some accommodation. Level 1 is frequently — and misleadingly — described as "high functioning," a label that conflates visibility with need. Many Level 1 autistic people have significant support needs that are invisible precisely because they've spent years masking effectively. The absence of obvious impairment is not the same as the absence of real difficulty.

Level 2 ("requiring substantial support"): More marked difficulties in social communication across contexts, and restricted/repetitive behaviors that are noticeable and interfere with functioning in multiple areas.

Level 3 ("requiring very substantial support"): Severe difficulties in social communication, significant distress when routines are disrupted, and restricted/repetitive behaviors that substantially interfere with functioning across domains.

Two things worth knowing about this system: First, support needs are not static — the same person can present differently across environments, life stages, and stress levels. A person diagnosed Level 1 during a stable period may need substantially more support during burnout. Second, level assignments are based on observable presentation, which means masking directly affects how a person is leveled. A heavily masked autistic person may be assigned a lower level than reflects their actual experience or support needs. See also: Camouflaging; Autistic Burnout; Masking; Asperger's Syndrome (Historical).

Asperger's Syndrome (Historical)
#Autism

Asperger's Syndrome was a diagnostic category in the DSM-IV describing autistic people with average or above-average language development and IQ — roughly corresponding to what is now called Level 1 Autism Spectrum Disorder under the DSM-5 (APA, 2013). The diagnosis was retired in 2013 when the DSM-5 consolidated all autism subtypes into a single spectrum diagnosis.

Two reasons the term became contested, beyond the diagnostic consolidation: First, the category had been used — by clinicians, institutions, and popular culture — to imply a fundamentally less impaired form of autism, which didn't hold up. The support needs of people previously diagnosed with Asperger's varied enormously, and "you seem too functional to be really autistic" became a sentence many of them heard regularly, with real consequences for their access to support. Second, historical research published by Herwig Czech in 2018 documented Hans Asperger's active collaboration with the Nazi eugenics program during the 1930s and 40s, including referring disabled children to a clinic where they were killed. This has led much of the autistic advocacy community to reject the term on ethical grounds.

Some adults diagnosed before 2013 continue to identify with the Asperger's label — as a matter of personal history, diagnostic accuracy at the time, or community identity. That's a legitimate choice. The clinical landscape has moved on; the individual relationship with the term is the person's to determine. See also: Autism Diagnostic Levels (DSM-5); Camouflaging; Late Diagnosis.

ABA (Applied Behavior Analysis)
#Autism

A behavioral intervention derived from B.F. Skinner's operant conditioning principles, historically the most widely prescribed autism treatment — particularly intensive early childhood versions (Lovaas therapy / discrete trial training). ABA uses reinforcement systems to increase target behaviors and reduce others. Its stated goals have historically included increasing eye contact, reducing stimming, improving compliance, and producing behaviors that appear neurotypical. Supporters cite skill acquisition research; critics — including the majority of the autistic self-advocacy community — argue that ABA suppresses natural regulatory behaviors (stimming, authentic communication), increases masking, and in its historical forms constituted behavioral suppression of autistic identity under the framing of therapy. A significant body of autistic adult testimony describes lasting psychological harm from intensive ABA exposure, including trauma responses. The debate is active and the research is genuinely contested. Clinically: many autistic clients carry ABA history, and that history deserves the same weight as any other formative experience that shaped a person's relationship with their own behavior, self-expression, and compliance.

Broader Autism Phenotype (BAP)
#Autism

A cluster of personality and cognitive traits that resemble autism in character but are milder in expression — present and recognizable, but below the threshold required for a formal diagnosis. BAP traits include reduced social drive, preference for routine, attention to detail, tendency toward literal interpretation, and reduced interest in reciprocal social interaction. People in the BAP range may experience the world in ways that closely resemble autistic experience — may recognize themselves deeply in autistic frameworks, find the same environments overwhelming, and benefit from the same accommodations — without ever meeting or being given a diagnosis. Clinically, this matters: diagnostic thresholds are imprecise instruments, and the absence of a formal diagnosis says nothing about whether autistic-informed approaches are appropriate or useful. The BAP also appears at higher rates in first-degree relatives of autistic people, suggesting shared neurological architecture.

Cognitive Dynamism
#ADHD #Autism

Cognitive dynamism is the ND tendency toward rapid, non-linear, intuitive problem-solving — the ability to see an angle on a problem that methodical thinkers might reach eventually, if at all. It's what makes some people with ADHD and autism extraordinarily effective in fast-moving, ambiguous, high-stakes environments. The same brain that resists a tedious spreadsheet can produce genuinely original thinking when the conditions are right and the stakes are real. I also like to refer to this as cognitive abundance — a reframe that emphasizes what's actually present rather than what's different.

Divergent Thinking (as Asset)
#ADHD #Autism

When the ND tendency to generate multiple responses, question default assumptions, and notice unexpected connections gets pointed at a problem worth solving, it's a genuine advantage. The challenge isn't generating the ideas — it's building enough structure around the process that the ideas have somewhere to land. The capacity itself is a real asset, not a consolation prize for attention difficulties.

Hyperfocus (as Asset)
#ADHD

When hyperfocus lands on something that matters — a creative project, a problem worth solving, a domain that activates genuine interest — it produces a depth of engagement and quality of output that's genuinely hard to replicate through sustained effort alone. The challenge isn't generating this state; it's directing it. Building a life with enough ICNU-rich work that hyperfocus shows up where it's useful — that's the actual design question.

Pattern Recognition
#ADHD #Autism

Pattern recognition is the capacity to find structure in noise, identify recurring dynamics, and see connections across domains that aren't obviously related. ND brains often do this intuitively and rapidly. It underlies strong diagnostic reasoning, creative synthesis, and the ability to notice what everyone else has been too close to see. It's also why many ND people pick up on social and emotional dynamics that the people directly involved have completely missed.

Creativity
#ADHD #Autism

Creativity in ND brains isn't just divergent thinking under a different name — it's a distinct capacity worth naming separately. Where divergent thinking describes the ability to generate multiple responses to a problem, creativity describes what happens when that capacity combines with deep domain knowledge, intrinsic motivation, and the ND tendency to notice what others have filtered out. The result is not just more ideas, but ideas that cross domains, resist obvious framings, and arrive from angles that more convergent thinkers wouldn't reach.

Research on creativity and neurodivergence points to a few overlapping mechanisms: reduced latent inhibition — the brain's tendency to filter out seemingly irrelevant stimuli — means ND brains admit more raw material into conscious processing. What gets filtered as noise in most systems stays available as signal. Combine that with the depth of engagement that characterizes special interests and hyperfocus, and you get a processing style genuinely well-suited to original synthesis.

The challenge is rarely generative capacity. It's the infrastructure around it: the ability to capture ideas when they arrive, to develop them past the initial spark, and to complete them through the parts that don't carry the same activation charge as the beginning. ND creativity is real. The bottleneck is almost never the idea. See also: Divergent Thinking; Hyperfocus (as Asset); Special Interest; Pattern Recognition.

ND in High-Control Environments
#ADHD #Autism

High-control environments — organizations, institutions, families, or relationships structured around rigid hierarchy, demand compliance, punish ambiguity, and offer little tolerance for deviation from expected behavior — are among the most consistently difficult contexts for neurodivergent people to survive in, let alone thrive.

The reasons compound. Masking demands are highest where conformity expectations are most explicit. Autonomy — which ND nervous systems often require to access genuine engagement and self-regulation — is structurally limited. Unpredictability is often wielded deliberately as a control mechanism: arbitrary rule changes, inconsistent enforcement, unexplained decisions from above. Injustice sensitivity, common in both autism and ADHD, is activated constantly and has nowhere to go. And the performance expectations in these environments are almost always built around neurotypical executive function — punctuality, linear task completion, affect regulation under pressure, compliance without visible resistance — leaving little room for the actual shape of an ND nervous system.

What this produces over time is not a character problem. It's a load problem: the chronic demand of performing neurotypicality under high scrutiny, in a system that provides few of the conditions ND brains need to function, and that tends to interpret ND responses to those conditions as attitude, insubordination, or incapacity. Many ND people who present with occupational burnout, unresolved shame about their work performance, or a history of terminations they don't fully understand have a high-control environment history at the center of it. Naming the environment as a variable — not just the person's response to it — is often the first useful reframe.
